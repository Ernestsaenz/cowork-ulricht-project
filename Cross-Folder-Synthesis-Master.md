**This is a coordinated international research consortium** — not three independent archives. ## B. Research and Funding Landscape Synthesis

### Common Biomedical Themes

1. **Digital health for underserved populations:** All three folders address healthcare access gaps through technology — AI clinical decision support (AICOMTA, EVAH) and telemedicine service delivery (EKFS).

2. **Primary care strengthening:** The AICOMTA tool targets primary care physicians; the EKFS project targets frontline health workers in ethnic health organizations; the EVAH concept targets community health workers. All aim to extend specialist-level knowledge to non-specialist providers.

3. **Health equity framing:** Rural and remote populations, conflict-affected communities, ethnic minorities, and resource-limited settings are central to all three. The Spanish HCV retrieval study explicitly targets patients lost to follow-up — a health equity issue.

4. **Clinical governance and safety:** AICOMTA's specialist-validated content model and the EVAH concept's safety monitoring emphasis both address the critical challenge of ensuring AI-generated clinical recommendations are trustworthy and safe.

5. **Myanmar as focal geography:** Two of three folders directly target Myanmar. The country represents a strategic convergence: severe healthcare access challenges (post-coup, conflict-affected), established telemedicine infrastructure (Telekyanmar), and receptive ethnic health organizations as implementation partners.

### Common Grant-Development Patterns

1. **Multi-partner international consortia:** All proposals involve cross-national teams (Spain–Germany–Myanmar, plus Latin America in expansion plans). This reflects the reality of global health research funding but creates coordination complexity.

2. **Staged evidence generation:** The AICOMTA folder shows a logical progression from protocol → pilot → validation → publication → expansion funding. The EKFS application builds on 3+ years of operational telemedicine data. The EVAH concept proposes Pathway A (early evaluation) as precursor to Pathway B (impact evaluation). All teams understand the need for incremental evidence building.

3. **Technology-first, then evaluation:** All three share a "build it, deploy it, then evaluate it" approach rather than the traditional research model of hypothesis → controlled trial → clinical practice. This is appropriate for digital health interventions but creates tension with funders expecting traditional research rigor.

4. **Pharmaceutical/Foundation funding mix:** The AICOMTA project combines pharma partnerships (AbbVie, Novo Nordisk, AstraZeneca, Gilead) with foundation/prize funding (Zendal). The EKFS application is pure foundation funding. The EVAH opportunity combines three major foundations (Wellcome, Gates, Novo Nordisk). This funding diversity is strategically sound but requires careful management of IP, open-access, and conflict-of-interest policies.

### Key Recurring Omissions Across All Folders

1. **Cost-effectiveness analysis:** None of the three folders includes or proposes a health economic evaluation. For a consortium working on digital health deployment, this is a significant gap — funders and ministries of health increasingly require cost data for adoption decisions.

2. **Patient-reported outcomes:** All proposals measure provider-facing metrics (usability, satisfaction, consultation counts) but none incorporate patient experience, patient-reported outcomes, or patient safety from the patient perspective.

3. **Regulatory pathway documentation:** None of the three folders addresses medical device classification, regulatory approval, or software-as-medical-device (SaMD) considerations for AICOMTA. As AI tools move toward clinical use, this becomes a compliance and scalability requirement.

4. **Comparative effectiveness:** None compares AICOMTA to existing clinical decision support tools (UpToDate, Clinical Key, WHO guidelines apps). This is needed to demonstrate added value beyond existing alternatives.

### Opportunities to Consolidate and Strengthen

1. **Unified consortium narrative:** The three folders can be presented as a coherent research program: *"Development (Spain) → Infrastructure (Myanmar/EKFS) → Evaluation (Myanmar/EVAH) → Scale-up (Latin America/Zendal)"*. This narrative strengthens each individual application by showing it sits within a larger, strategic, evidence-building arc.

2. **Cross-funding synergies:** If the EKFS application is funded, it secures the telemedicine infrastructure needed for the EVAH evaluation. The EVAH evaluation, if funded, provides the rigorous evidence needed for AICOMTA's Latin American expansion. The Zendal Prize provides visibility and seed funding for the Latin American arm. These can be explicitly positioned as complementary, non-duplicative investments.

3. **Shared preliminary data:** The AICOMTA validation results (4.2/5 expert quality rating, 87.5% adoption intent, 328 clinical conversations) and the Telekyanmar operational data (190,000 consultations, 86% satisfaction) can both be cited as preliminary evidence in the EVAH application.

4. **Combined team strength:** Dr. Turnes (AICOMTA technology), Dr. Kuch (Myanmar telemedicine operations), and the Heidelberg team (evaluation methodology) together form a stronger consortium than any individual partner. The EVAH application should explicitly position this three-way expertise triangle.

5. **Disease scope expansion:** The gastroenterology/endocrinology focus is well-justified for Spain but may limit competitiveness for Myanmar-focused funding (where malaria, TB, maternal health, respiratory infections are higher burden). The team should consider either broadening AICOMTA's knowledge base to include these conditions, or providing epidemiological data justifying the gastro/endocrinology focus in Myanmar.
